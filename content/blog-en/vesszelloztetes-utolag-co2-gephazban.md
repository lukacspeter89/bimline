We were called in as designers for the transcritical CO₂ (R744) machinery room of a downtown Budapest grocery store. The task sounded simple at first: emergency ventilation had to be built, because there was none. The survey then revealed that the list of deficiencies was longer — and that the situation is not a one-off, but systemic.

## What we found on site

The machinery room was a typical case of modern commercial refrigeration: a large-charge, state-of-the-art system in a room that was never designed as a machinery room. The refrigeration replacement made the room a machinery room in function — but bringing the room itself up to standard was left out. The survey report recorded the following:

- there was **no mechanical emergency ventilation** and **no make-up air path**;
- the enclosure was **not gas-tight**;
- of the **two mandatory manual emergency controls, only one** was present.

Meanwhile, the system cooled flawlessly. That is the most important lesson of this situation: these deficiencies **cause no malfunction**, so they never surface on their own. The store can operate like this for years — the gap only becomes visible in a survey, or in an incident.

## The numbers

The machinery room held a **120 kg CO₂ charge** on a floor area of **13.6 m²**. MSZ EN 378-3 derives the required emergency ventilation airflow from the refrigerant charge (the 0.014 × m²ᐟ³ formula, in m³/s) — here that means an extract airflow of approximately **1,230 m³/h**, with a continuous make-up air path.

CO₂ is heavier than air; in case of a leak it accumulates near the floor, and in a small, enclosed machinery room it can reach dangerous concentrations quickly. This is why neither gas detection nor emergency ventilation is a formality — while CO₂ systems are precisely the ones that fall outside the regular, documented external inspections that the F-gas Regulation (EU 517/2014) prescribes for fluorinated refrigerants. CO₂ is not an F-gas, so it has no mandatory leak-check cycle — there is nothing to bring the deficiencies to the surface.

## The key to the solution was a site condition

The extracted air must be discharged above roof level — in a downtown multi-storey building this is typically the most expensive question. Opening a new shaft through several floors means slab penetrations, fire-rated crossings, coordination with every affected level, and weeks to months of time.

In this case the survey showed that an **existing ventilation riser could be reused** for the emergency ventilation. Opening a new shaft could thus be avoided entirely — an order-of-magnitude saving in cost and time. This is exactly the decision that cannot be made from the desk: only an on-site survey can tell whether an existing riser is suitable.

## Why this is not a one-off case

We believe the situation above applies to a significant share of the installed base, for three reasons:

1. **Most modern refrigeration systems were retrofitted into existing buildings.** The plant room was never built as a machinery room, and bringing it up to standard was often left out of the replacement project.
2. **CO₂ systems have no mandatory external inspection cycle** that would sooner or later surface the deficiencies — while the personal risk is highest precisely here.
3. **The gap can only be identified by an on-site survey**, and the remedy is site-specific too — as in this project, where the existing riser determined the order of magnitude of the costs.

The same logic applies to VRF systems: the ratio of refrigerant charge to the volume of the smallest occupied room is regularly exceeded in offices, hotels and meeting rooms, and with A2L refrigerants (e.g. R32) flammability requirements apply as well.

Precise wording matters: EN 378 is a standard, not a law, so there is no direct "EN 378 fine". The absence of a standard-compliant installation is, however, a risk that falls within the responsibility of the employer and the operator, enforced indirectly through health-and-safety obligations, national fire-safety regulations, and contractual and insurance conditions.

## Summary

The project closed with the store's machinery room demonstrably meeting the requirements of the standard — and the client knowing exactly what was installed and why. The lesson, from an operator's perspective, is simple: the question is not whether the system cools, but whether the machinery room can do what a machinery room must. A survey and a compliance report settle that question.
